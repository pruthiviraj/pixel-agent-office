# I Built a Tiny Pixel Office Where AI Agents Do the Work — and You Can Watch

We've all heard that "AI will change how we build software." But most of the time, that change is invisible. You type a prompt into a chatbox, something happens behind a curtain, and you get an answer back. It feels like magic — but it also feels like a black box.

So I tried something different. I asked a simple question:

**What if you could *watch* a team of AI assistants work — like watching people through the window of an office?**

That idea turned into a project I call **Pixel Agent Office**.

---

## The big idea, in plain words

Imagine you walk up to a little retro, video-game-style office on your screen. Inside, there's a small team of characters:

- A **manager** who takes your goal and breaks it into a to-do list.
- A few **builders** who each pick up a task and start working.
- A **reviewer** who checks every finished piece and gives it a thumbs up or thumbs down.

You give the office one plain-English instruction — something like *"build me a discount system for my store"* — and then you sit back and watch the team get to work. The manager plans. The builders build, several at once. The reviewer inspects each result. If something isn't good enough, it goes right back to the builder with a note explaining what to fix.

No curtain. No black box. Just a little office you can watch, the same way you'd glance across a room and see who's busy and who's stuck.

---

## Why I think this matters

Three things make this more than just a fun animation:

**1. You can see what's happening.**
Instead of trusting that "the AI is working," you literally watch tasks move across the room — from *planned*, to *being built*, to *being reviewed*, to *done*. It turns an abstract process into something you can follow at a glance.

**2. Quality is built in.**
Nothing is called "finished" just because one assistant said so. Every piece of work gets checked by a separate reviewer before it counts. And here's the part I love: when something fails, the office **remembers the lesson** — so the team stops making the same mistake twice, both within a project and across future ones. It quietly gets better over time.

**3. You stay in control.**
This isn't a "press start and hope" tool. While the team works, you can pause it, stop it, ask someone to redo a task, or overrule the reviewer when you know better. And because real work costs real money, there's a live meter showing the running cost — plus a **budget limit** you can set, like a spending cap on a card. When it hits the limit, it wraps up cleanly instead of running away with your wallet.

---

## A window for the whole team

One of the surprises was how useful the "office view" is for *people*, not just for the AI.

You can put it up on a screen in a standup meeting and everyone instantly understands the status. You can replay a past project like a recording to see how it went. It became less of a tool and more of a **shared dashboard** — a way to make AI work visible to a team that's used to it happening out of sight.

---

## It's also careful by default

I'm well aware that handing a goal to a team of AI workers and walking away sounds a little scary. So the project is cautious on purpose:

- It works on a **separate copy** of your project, never the original, so your real work is protected.
- It refuses to start in risky situations, and undoing everything is a single step.
- It never pushes anything live to your customers — that decision always stays with a human.

The philosophy is simple: let the AI do the heavy lifting, but keep a person's hand on the wheel.

---

## The bigger picture

What started as a playful idea — *make the AI visible* — turned into something genuinely useful. We talk a lot about AI "taking over tasks," but trust comes from being able to *see* and *steer* what's happening. A black box you can't watch is hard to trust. An office you can look into, pause, and correct? That's something you can actually work alongside.

I think that's the direction worth chasing: not AI that hides what it does, but AI that works **in the open**, with people watching, guiding, and stepping in when it counts.

The project is open and free for anyone to try, fork, and adapt. If you build something fun with it, I'd genuinely love to hear about it.
